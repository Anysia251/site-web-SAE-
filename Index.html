Charging stations in Allier: meeting the growing needs of electric vehicles by 2030.

With the energy transition and the rise of electric mobility, charging infrastructure for electric vehicles (EV) is becoming a crucial issue for the regions, particularly in Allier. As in many rural regions in France, the municipalities of this department are confronted to a major challenge: developing enough charging stations to respond to the rapid increase in electric vehicles in circulation. At this point, the question is: is the current number of charging stations sufficient to satisfy the needs of EV drivers? And how will this distribution have to evolve by 2030 to support the planned growth of the electricity fleet?

Unequal distribution of infrastructure

The table of charging stations and electric vehicles in the main municipalities of Allier for 2023-2024 reveals significant disparities. For example, Montluçon, the most populous municipality, has 19 charging points for 443 electric vehicles, a ratio of one terminal for approximately 23 vehicles. Vichy, with 6 sockets for 322 electric vehicles, displays a much less favorable ratio: around 54 vehicles per terminal. Towns like Yzeure (11 sockets for 228 vehicles) or Moulins (8 sockets for 261 vehicles) are located in an intermediate zone.

These gaps reveal increasing pressure on public terminals, particularly in cities where the EV fleet is growing faster than charging infrastructure. If certain municipalities like Montluçon are relatively well equipped, others, like Toulon-sur-Allier, present a paradox: with 18 sockets for only 24 electric vehicles, this small municipality benefits from a surplus of infrastructure compared to its population. current VE. Conversely, cities such as Vichy, which attract large numbers of visitors, are struggling to meet growing demand.

A statistical analysis of infrastructure

To fully understand the extent of these disparities, a statistical examination of charging stations and the number of electric vehicles in Allier reveals the following points:

- Average number of terminals per city: 7.14 - Average number of electric vehicles in 2024: 114.43 - Standard deviation of the number of terminals: 5.53, indicating great variability between municipalities. - Median charging stations: 4 terminals (which means that half of the municipalities have less than 4 terminals for more than 55 vehicles on average).

In 2024, certain cities will therefore find themselves in a situation of undercapacity. Take the example of Montluçon: with its 19 terminals and a fleet of 443 EVs, the ratio remains relatively sustainable, but if the fleet were to double by 2030, as projected, the city would quickly face a shortage of charging stations . In smaller municipalities like Lapalisse or Commentry, which only have 4 terminals each, the situation is even more worrying

Exponential growth in the number of electric vehicles

The evolution of the electric vehicle fleet in Allier is impressive. In 2020, there were 933 EVs throughout the department. In 2024, this figure jumped to 2,403. If the French government achieves its goal of banning the sale of thermal vehicles by 2035, growth is expected to accelerate over the next decade. By 2030, the number of EVs in Allier could easily double or even triple.

By applying this projection to Montluçon, for example, the number of EVs could reach 900 units by 2030. However, with only 19 sockets available today, the municipality will have to invest quickly to avoid undercapacity. Small towns like Lapalisse, which currently only have 4 terminals, risk becoming real “electrical deserts” if we do not anticipate a more rapid expansion of infrastructure.

Optimizing the distribution of charging stations: an imperative for 2030

Faced with this inevitable growth, it is urgent to optimize the distribution of charging stations in Allier. Several actions can be considered:

Concentration of infrastructure in urban areas: Cities like Montluçon and Vichy, which concentrate a significant share of EVs, must benefit as a priority from new terminals to meet growing local demand. Particular attention should also be paid to tourist towns such as Saint-Pourçain-sur-Sioule. Development in rural areas: Although the number of EVs is limited in small communities, it is essential to anticipate the long-term transition. Cities like Ebreuil and Doyet, which currently only have one socket each, will need to develop their infrastructure to encourage the adoption of EVs. Encourage the establishment of private charging stations and community initiatives: The installation of private terminals in homes, particularly in rural areas, could ease the pressure on public infrastructure. Municipalities should also encourage local businesses and establishments to set up terminals accessible to the general public.

Improve fast charging stations: Although their cost is high, these stations can recharge a vehicle in less than 30 minutes, which encourages greater user turnover and reduces pressure on infrastructure.

The current number of charging stations in Allier is far from sufficient to meet the growing needs of electric vehicles on the roads. With a stock that has grown from 933 in 2020 to 2403 in 2024, the infrastructure must imperatively adapt to this trend. Allier will have to establish a bold policy by 2030, combining the development of terminals in urban and rural areas. It is crucial to invest massively and rebalance infrastructure to avoid saturation of terminals and ensure a smooth transition to electric mobility in the years to come.
